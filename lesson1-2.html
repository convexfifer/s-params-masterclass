Lesson 1-2: Understanding S-Parameters

In the last section, we looked at, so what? Why do we care about S-parameters? And what we

discovered was that S-parameters are the medium of exchange of information about the electrical

properties of an interconnect. Everything we ever wanted to know about the electrical

properties of an interconnect are contained inside the S-parameters. And that's why they're

so valuable because we can use them as a behavioral model of that physical interconnect.

Now let's talk about, okay, so what are S-parameters? That S in S-parameters stands for scattering

because that's really how we get the S-parameters from an interconnect. The formalism for S-parameters

was originally developed using sine waves as a very well-characterized signal. We send

that signal incident into our interconnect, and we look at how that signal scatters off

of the interconnect. Either it scatters back at us or scatters through the interconnect to

some other entrance or exit of the device. When we talk about S-parameters, then, it's

really about how does that interconnect treat sine waves. And all of the formulas that we're

going to talk about applies to interconnects which are linear, passive, and time-invariant.

The linear part means that if we send a signal in at one volt, we get a certain response coming

up. If we send a signal in at two volts, we're going to get double that response, not three-halves,

not 67%, but it's going to be exactly double. That means that if we send one sine wave in

at a frequency of a gigahertz, for example, we're only going to get one sine wave out at

one gigahertz. A non-linear device, on the other hand, would be a device that you send in

gigahertz. And you're going to get one gigahertz and maybe two or three or four or five gigahertz

harmonics of that signal. So for the interconnect structures that we're going to be dealing with,

we're going to assume that they are linear devices. And for 99% of all the interconnect

structures out there, they're all linear structures. The second term of passive means that we don't have

gain. That just means that we send a signal in, the maximum voltage we're going to get out

is going to be that input input. We're never going to get more than we put in. And the third property

of time invariant means that during the time we're doing the measurement of simulation,

the interconnect physically is not changing. That means that it doesn't matter whether we take a minute

or a microsecond in order to do the measurement of simulation. During that time, the interconnect

is constant. That means that every time we do the measurement, we're going to get the same values

for the S parameters. While the formalism was developed in the frequency domain,

because we're dealing with linear passive time invariant structures, we can actually apply

those sine wave behaviors to any kind of waveform going in. And one of the common transforms that

we're going to do is look at the behavior of the S parameters in the time domain with a step edge.

We sometimes refer to that as TDR, time domain reflectometry. And it's not just about the

reflected signals, it's also about the transmitted signal. So sometimes we refer to it as TDT,

time domain transmitted signal. But the behaviors are identical. We're going to send a well-characterized

signal in, we're going to look at how it scatters off of the device. Whether it is sine waves,

or it is a step edge, if we know one, we can figure out what the other is, just with a little bit

of algebra, as long as we have linear passive time invariant structures. Now we can look at,

well, how are we going to actually go about getting the S parameters from a device? And there are two

general ways. The first is by measurement, the second is by simulation. The typical instrument

used to measure the S parameters device is a vector network analyzer, or VNA. That vector part just means

that we're going to be paying attention to not just the magnitude of the sine wave that reflects,

but also the impact on the phase of that sine wave that goes in. And so we're going to pay attention

to that vector, the amplitude change and the phase change of the sine wave from what goes into what

comes out. This is an example of a couple of network analyzers, and I show them for particular

reasons. The very first network analyzer was the 8410 introduced by HP back in 1967. This sent sine waves

in measured the amplitude and phase change, and this first one went up to about the 12 gigahertz

kind of range. The 8510 was introduced in 1985, and the reason I show this is because this is what I

learned S parameters on. This was the very first network analyzer that I had a chance to have hands-on

experience with, and I learned everything I know about S parameters from Tony Wade, who was the application

engineer at the time that sat me in front of an 8510 and taught me about S parameters. So a lot of what I'm

telling you today is what I learned from Tony back in 1985. And this is an example of a more modern

network analyzer. Again, very similar. It's going to do exactly the same thing. It's going to send a

sine wave in. It's going to measure the change in that sine wave's amplitude and phase because of

its scattering off the device under test. This is an example of using measurements as that medium of

exchange of information about the electrical properties of the interconnect. In addition, we can also

use simulation, and there are generally two categories of simulation tools. There are circuit simulators

that use ideal circuit elements like transmission line elements or R's and L's and C's, and we can use

circuit simulators in order to simulate the behavior of those circuits and how sine waves are treated by

those circuits, and that's one way of getting S parameters. Another way is using an electromagnetic field

solver. And in a field solver, we're looking at the boundary conditions of the geometry, the materials,

and the dielectrics, and we're solving maps of those equations. And again, we're sending sine waves in

as electromagnetic fields entering into the device. We're calculating all those fields and how those

fields going in, the propagating fields going in, are changed by the interconnect and give us a change in

the input and phase of the signals coming out. So even though it is a field solver, it's calculated

the electric and magnetic fields dynamically in the device under test due to the interaction with all

the conductors and the dielectrics. Even though it's doing all that, it's still calculating for us

the behavior of sine waves and giving us the S parameters. Inside, under the hood, are all the

electromagnetic field calculations, but externally the output of the field solver are going to be

S parameters. And whether they come from a measurement or simulation, they're still the S parameters.

And sometimes we call the simulation environment a virtual twin or a virtual prototype of the real

thing. And we use the network analyzer to measure the S parameters of the real thing. In order to

think about the S parameters and understand them, there are a couple of important principles to pay

attention to in the definition of the S parameters. The first is this idea of a port. Any device under test

that we're going to look at, in order to send sine waves in, we have to have a connection to it.

And because they are signals, we need a connection between the signal and return path of the

conductor. There are no exceptions. There is always a signal and a return path to the device under test.

And a simple way of thinking about that connection we call a port to the device under test is think of

it as a little coax cable. Think of it that way because a coax cable has a signal and a return path

on it. And in a coax, you can have two waves propagating, one going in, one coming out, one

going to the right, one going to the left. And that's exactly what happens when we either do a

measurement or simulation of the S parameters of the device under test. We're going to send a sine wave

in and we're going to look at what reflects back. And so in that one interconnect path, we're going to

have two waves, one traveling to the right, one traveling to the left. And how we either do the

simulation of the measurement, we have to have a way of separating what is the wave propagating in,

so we know the incident wave, and what is the wave propagating out, so we know the, in this case,

it would be the reflected wave. Every connection to the device under test in which we're going to want

to see how does that wave scatter, either going in and coming back or scattering through, every

connection of the device is going to be a port. And here's an example of a real device. This is a

breakout board for, this is an HDMI cable. It's got a number of ports on it. Each of them are

literally little coax cables. There's going to be a signal and a return connection to each of these.

We're going to send a signal in. We're going to look at what comes back. And if we send a signal to

one of these, we can look at the signal that comes out of some of the others. There are a couple of

other important features of the port based on this idea of thinking of as a coax cable. We've got the

signal return path connection. We're going to have both the incident and the transmitted signal,

the reflected signal, that are both propagating in that cable at the same time. They don't interfere

with each other. And there's also a port impedance. In other words, this signal that's traveling into

this coax cable, before it hits the device under test, it is in a particular impedance environment.

There's a characteristic impedance to this coax cable connection. That signal that's propagating

down that coax to get into the device, where it enters the device where that port is. It is in a

particular impedance environment. We call that the port impedance. The value of the port impedance

is just as important as the impedance of the device under test, because that's going to influence us.

That impedance change that influences the reflected signal, for example, is going to be the difference

in impedance between the port impedance and the device under test. And generally, unless it's

otherwise specified, that port impedance is going to be 50 ohms. When we do a measurement or simulation,

we're generally going to use a 50 ohm port impedance, so we know what that input impedance is

that the signal sees right before it enters the device under test. And likewise, if we have another port

somewhere else, one of these other ports, and a signal is coming out of the device under test,

the port impedance of that connection to the device between its signal and return, its output,

signal return, that impedance is also going to be 50 ohms. Now comes the confusing part of if we have

one port on the device under test, keeping track of the going in and coming out, that's easy. It's one port.

But as soon as we have multiple ports connecting to the device, we need to keep track of all those

combinations of a going in and a coming out. Which port do we have the signal going into? Which port do we have the

signal coming out of? And we're going to look at all combinations. And each S parameter is really going

to be one of those combinations. It is literally going to be the ratio of the sine wave that comes

out of some port compared to the sine wave going in. And of course, when we take the ratio of two sine

waves, we have to pay attention to the ratio of the amplitudes. That's going to be a magnitude. And we

have to pay attention to the phase change between the coming out and the going in. And that means that

each S parameter is going to be a complex number. It's going to have a magnitude and phase or a real

and imaginary part. And it's going to vary with frequency. That is a lot of information to keep

track of. And that's part of where the formalism comes in. It's keeping track of all of that

information. And we do that using port numbers or index numbers. And here's how to think about the

index number. So I've got a kind of a device under test here that's got four ports. We

label the port identifications in terms of just numbers, starting with one and going up for however

many ports that we have. We're going to send signals in because after all, S parameters are

about the behavior of signals, in particular, sine waves. What are the properties of a sine wave? A

sine wave has a frequency, it has an amplitude, and it has a phase. Those are the three properties of

the sine wave that goes in. We want to keep track of what port did it go into, what port does it come

out of, and what is the change in the amplitude and the phase and the frequency? Well, because we're

dealing with linear passive time invariant devices, the frequency is not going to change. One gigahertz

goes in, one gigahertz comes out. And so we really only have to keep track of the change in amplitude

and the change in phase of those sine waves. And to keep track of which port is going in and which

is coming out, that's where the index numbers come in. And so when we describe a sine wave going in on

port one and the sine wave coming back on port one, that's the reflective signal, we're going to refer

to that particular combination of sine waves in terms of the port going in as one and the port coming

out as one, and that we label as \(S_{11}\). Another term for \(S_{11}\) we'll talk about in the next section

is return loss. So \(S_{11}\) is a measure of the reflected signal. Similarly, it's called the

reflection coefficient. If we send a signal into port one and we want to look at what happens to

that signal in scattering through the device to port two, then we refer to that as \(S_{21}\). But wait a

minute, wouldn't it make a lot more sense? It went from one to two, so wouldn't it make more sense if we

call that \(S_{12}\)? Because it goes from port one to port two. Yeah, it would, but it screws up the

mathematical formalism, because the basis of S parameters is a lot of matrix math. And in using

that matrix math, we have to adopt the formalism of the labeling scheme of the S parameters,

consistent with the matrix math. And part of that matrix math says, okay, when we label the S

parameters, we're going to do it in the exact opposite way. The first number is the coming out

port. The second number is the going in port. And so we go in on port one, we come out on port two.

So the two is the first, the one is the second. And this is one of the most confusing parts about S

parameters, is that the labeling scheme is the exact opposite of what our intuition would tell us.

Instead of an \(S_{12}\), going from one to two, it's the opposite. It's an S coming out to one going in.

And that's going to be the case for all the S parameters. And so for the transmitted signal going

through the device under test, that's going to be \(S_{21}\). And we sometimes refer to that through path

S parameters that direct through, through an interconnect, we sometimes refer to that as insertion

and then we've got the other S parameters depending on how many ports we have. Looking at all the

different ports that we have, we're going to have going ins and coming out every combination.

And so if we send a signal into port one and we look at what comes out of port three,

we would label that as \(S_{31}\). And we had a signal going into port one and coming out port four,

we would label that as \(S_{41}\). And so now you begin to see that labeling scheme. This way we can keep

track of absolutely every combination of a going in and a coming out. And if we have four ports,

how many combinations are there? They're going to be, you can go in on port one, you can come out on

port one, come out on port two, port three, port four. So for every port, n number of outputs. And so

for n ports, they're going to be n squared different S parameters. So we have four ports, that means we

have 16 different S parameters. Each one is going to be the ratio of the coming out to the going in,

based on the port number. And each one of these ratios is going to be the ratio of two sine waves,

the sine wave coming out compared to going in. And it's going to be frequency dependent. It's going

to change as we change the frequency. And to keep track of that ratio of sine wave coming out compared

to the sine wave going in, that ratio, the way we take the ratio to sine waves is as a complex number.

It's going to be the ratio of the amplitudes of what comes out compared to what goes in,

and the phase difference between what comes out compared to what goes in. And we can describe

each of the S parameters as a complex number. That complex number has magnitude and phase,

or it's real and imaginary, and it's going to vary with frequency. And it's going to depend on which

is the input and which is the output port. You can see there's a huge amount of information we have

to keep track of. And that's where the formalism and the labeling scheme is so important. So we can

refer to each S parameter absolutely unambiguously. So what are the S parameters?

They're a collection of numbers. They're going to be the reflected waves that send a wave in and what

comes back, or they're going to be the transmitted waves of what goes in on one port and comes out

some other port. And we're going to pay attention at every frequency. And at every frequency,

each one of these S parameter terms is going to have a different value. It's going to be a complex

number. Keeping track of all that information, we use a special file format. And that format is

sometimes referred to as the touchstone format. The touchstone is a, it used to be an informal

industry spec. And now the touchstone spec has been taken over by the IBIS committee. It's in a

couple of different revs, but the basic format is the same for all touchstone files. And as long as

we all agree that when we create an S parameter file, we use this file format, then all the

different tools can read the S parameters. So whether it comes from a network analyzer, a measurement of

device, or it comes from a circuit simulator or an electromagnetic field solver, if the output is

in a touchstone format, all the tools can read them. And we interpret the S parameters exactly the same

way. When we open up the touchstone file, what we're going to see is, it's just a bunch of numbers.

The name of the touchstone file, the extension of the, of the touchstone file tells us how many

ports are we talking about. And that identifies how many different S parameter terms there are going

to be. If we have one port, that means we only have one in and one out, and that means there's only

one term of S parameters. The extension is going to be an S1P. The S says, stored in this file are S

parameters. There's one port, and the P just means how many ports that are there. This is going to be

the extension for the touchstone file. If there are two ports, that means we have a one, port one and

port two. So we have four combinations of going in and coming outs. Each one of those is going to be

frequency dependent. We're going to have four different terms. They're going to be complex

numbers. When we open up that touchstone file and look at it, it is literally just a text file. When

we open it up, we see that it has a special format to it. That's a comment line. It's just there for

reference. When we have a hashtag, that means contained in this line is special information.

And that's part of the touchstone format. If you open up a touchstone file, you'll see when you have a

hashtag. The first alphanumeric in there tells us the units for the frequency. All of the touchstone

file formats are the same. The first column is the frequency value. The second two columns are either

real and imaginary or magnitude and phase. And then each successive column, those pairs are the complex

numbers corresponding to each of the different S-parameter values. The first characters

in the hashtag tell us the units for frequency. The next character tells us what kind of information

is stored there because this is a generic format to store any frequency dependent complex number.

And so impedance, for example, frequency dependent complex numbers, especially as we have different

ports, we can use the touchstone format. And so if we were storing impedance information,

this would be a Z instead of an S. The second set of characters tells us what are the units for the

two different columns for that complex number. We can use, if it's dB, these are standard codes in

touchstone format. dB means the first magnitude is in units of dB. The second is the phase in degrees.

If this were RI, it would be, oh, we have real and imaginary as the two different column values. And there are a few other

different formats that we can use. The next term R means, okay, pay attention, this is the port impedance

from which these S-parameters were obtained. We can change that port impedance. And as long as we have a

tool that understands touchstone file formats, we can convert that port impedance from whatever it was

measured or simulated into any other port impedance using a little bit of algebra. So it's not critical

that we use a particular port impedance when we do the simulation or measurement, as long as we know

what it was, because then we can translate later into other port impedances. And so that's the decodering

to how to interpret the information containing it. But fundamentally, S-parameters are just a collection

of numbers. Well, based on the physics of what's going on and the behavior of the interactions of sine waves

with these devices, there is a rich formalism to understand how do the electrical properties of the
