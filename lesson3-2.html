Lesson 3-2: Crosstalk Analysis

So far, we've been looking at the patterns in the S-parameters. We started out looking at kind

of a two-port transmission line, a single-ended transmission line, and we looked at the return

and insertion loss, and we looked at the connection between the patterns in the return

loss with the ripples and the magnitude of the reflected signal and the insertion loss.

And we saw two characteristic patterns. We saw the ripples, and we saw an increase in the amplitude

of the ripples due to launch discontinuities. Then we added losses to the interconnect, and we saw the

monotonic drop-off in the insertion loss. And then we added yet another factor to the behavior of

transmission lines, and that was coupling to an adjacent line, and we introduced the ideas of

near-end and far-end crosstalk. And in that exploration of near-end and far-end crosstalk,

we saw a new kind of pattern emerge, which was a large dip in the insertion loss, and that was due

to energy coupling over to the adjacent line in the special case of coupled microstrip. Now we're

going to introduce two other sources of dips in the insertion loss, and we're going to look at the

root cause of those dips based on the behavior of ideal transmission lines. I'm going to walk you

through really briefly the idea of resonant coupling and how that's going to contribute to

narrow dips. We're going to see it in our digital twin, our simulation environment, and then we're

going to look at a couple of measured structures and see those same patterns. Here is basically the

circuit that we created when we looked at coupling in transmission lines. The way we describe the

coupling between two transmission lines is using the multilayer interconnect library element,

and we introduce the idea of a substrate that defines the stack up, and then in this case,

two transmission lines that have some coupling between them. We parameterize them in order to

look at the impact of the near-end and far-end crosstalk on the adjacent victim line. Now, I've

replaced the terminals that were on the end of the victim line with resistors so we could make their

resistance really high. This simulates the behavior of floating metal. And so we have two transmission

lines. We're going to drive the signal into the first one and look at the reflected and transmitted

signal. And we're going to get some energy coupled over to the victim line. Once we get a signal coupled

to the victim line, it's going to propagate in the victim line. It's going to hit the end of the line,

and I've added an open at the end of the line. And that signal is going to propagate back,

and it's going to bounce back and forth between the open ends of this line. This is a resonator,

and we're going to have energy that is resonating back and forth in this victim line. And of course,

as that signal bounces back and forth, it acts as an aggressor line to our first line,

and we're going to get some of this energy coupled back at the resonant frequency. And so we're going

to see behaviors in the aggressor line of energy being coupled out into the victim line at the resonant

frequency and some of the energy heading back and then heading in the source direction. And we're

going to see that as return loss. And in order to simulate that kind of behavior, we're going to use

that same structure that we had before, our coupled lines. We're going to drive that sine waves into the

line, and we're going to see that coupled energy between them. Let's get started first looking at

this setup. And you'll notice the situation that we have. This is just like we had before with two

reflection. So \(S_{11}\) is going to be a large negative dB value. And the insertion loss, we're going to see that

that. And then what we're going to do is we're going to change the length of coupling. We're

going to make it a shorter interconnect. Make it about one inch long. And we're going to bring the

two lines closer together. And we're going to look at the impact of that resonant coupling. And of

The spacing between the ripples is going to increase. And we have such a good match in this

Previously, when we looked at the return loss, we thought of return loss as due to reflections.

There's another source of signal coming back into port one that is not about reflection,

but about signal that's coupled back. Remember what is happening in our coupled line. We're

sending a signal in port one. It's traveling down to the other end. That's what we see over here.

And some of that energy, because of near end and far end crosstalk, is being coupled over to the

other line. That signal, when it gets to the other line, is going to propagate in both directions.

Some is going to hit the far end, which is open and reflect. Some is going to hit the near end,

So now let's turn off the dielectric loss. And if we turn off the dielectric loss, we see that we're able
